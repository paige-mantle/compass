**Goal:** Understand the alternatives, complaints, workarounds, and angles around your idea.

If people are already paying for a similar solution, that is not a threat. That is a market.

Competitors mean people already understand the problem, search for solutions, and spend money in the category. That's useful validation.

No competitors might seem like you found a perfect gap, but it usually means one of three things: the problem is not urgent enough, it is really hard to solve, or people are solving it in ways you have not thought of yet.

Looking at competitors is useful. Building a worse version of your competitor for $5 cheaper is not.

## Start with likely ecosystems

Before you start competitor research, write down the ecosystems where this app might realistically live.

You do not need to choose the platform yet. We'll do that in 1.5. For now, you just need to know where to look.

A tool for ecommerce stores might have competitors in the Shopify App Store, Wix App Market, WooCommerce/WordPress plugin directory, BigCommerce marketplace, Chrome Web Store, or standalone SaaS tools.

A tool for designers might show up as a Framer plugin, Figma plugin, Webflow app, Chrome extension, template, or standalone product.

The ecosystem changes the competitor set. A Shopify app and a Wix app might solve a similar problem, but the users, pricing, support expectations, and workflows can be totally different.

So don't just Google the broad app idea. Search where the customer would actually look.

## Look at direct competitors

Direct competitors are the products, apps, or solutions someone would immediately compare you against.

They usually solve the same problem in a similar way, with similar features, for a similar type of customer.

For example, if you're building a Shopify app for product bundles, your direct competitors are other Shopify bundle apps. If you're building a client deliverable tracker for freelance Framer designers, your direct competitors might be other client portal, project management, or deliverable-tracking tools aimed at freelancers.

Direct competitors might show up in the Shopify App Store, Chrome Web Store, Apple App Store, WordPress Plugin Directory, Framer/React/Notion template marketplaces, or wherever your customer already looks for tools.

Look at them, but don't obsess over them. The goal is to understand what customers already expect, what language the category uses, what features are table stakes, what customers complain about, and what still feels unresolved.

Homepages tell you what the company wants to be true. Reviews tell you what customers are actually experiencing.

## Look at indirect alternatives

You're not always competing with apps or other software products. You're competing with whatever the customer already does to make the pain go away.

That might be a spreadsheet, a template, a freelancer, a Zapier workflow, a manual process, a prompt, a VA, an internal tool, or doing nothing at all.

And now, you're also competing with AI.

Ask yourself: could someone solve this with ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, or a messy internal workflow before they pay for a standalone app? Could they build a good-enough version themselves?

I see a lot of app ideas thrown around in indie hacker and builder communities where my first thought is: Claude can just do this. Not because the idea is bad, but because the pain is not deep enough, the customer is too broad, or the app is not meaningfully better than the AI workaround.

That does not mean your idea is dead. It means your app needs to win in a way that actually matters: real data, repeatability, team safety, polish, ease of use, maintenance, or deeper workflow integration.

If the current workaround is annoying but good enough, your app has to be meaningfully easier, faster, safer, or more valuable to earn the switch.

<RealityCheck>

If Claude can do the whole thing in one prompt, you probably don't have an app yet. You have a workflow, a template, or a feature.

</RealityCheck>

## Read reviews and complaints

This is a totally underrated research strategy.

Look anywhere people complain: G2, Capterra, Shopify App Store reviews, Apple App Store reviews, Chrome extension reviews, Reddit, niche communities, Facebook groups, TikTok search, YouTube comments, support forums, comparison pages, and public feature requests.

When you can, sort reviews by two and three stars.

One-star reviews are often support meltdowns, bugs, or pricing rage. Five-star reviews are usually too polite, although sometimes people sneak real complaints into five-star reviews so they don't get deleted.

Two and three-star reviews are where the useful feedback usually lives. That's where people say, "I like this, but…" and then accidentally write your product brief.

Look for the same complaint showing up in different places: too expensive, too many bugs, missing features, bad support, hard setup, built for bigger companies now, or not flexible enough for the actual workflow. The repetition is the signal.

If the reviews are public and you can see the person's name, document that too. They might be someone worth researching later, or even reaching out to when you're validating the idea.

<Callout title="Proof to add later">

Add 2–3 real review snippets, Reddit quotes, or YouTube/TikTok comments here. The best ones show customers describing the same pain in their own words, not generic "this tool is bad" complaints.

</Callout>

## Find what customers already pay for

People already paying for products is a useful signal.

If your target end user or customer is already paying for three tools, a consultant, and a spreadsheet template to manage the problem, you are looking at a real budget.

If they complain loudly but have no budget and do not want to spend money, be careful. You'll see this with earlier-stage users in some ecosystems. For example, newer Shopify merchants may want low-cost apps and a lot of setup help because they're still learning how everything works.

That does not make them bad customers. It just means the support expectations and willingness to pay may not match the business you want to build.

The point is not to judge the customer. It is to understand the economics before you build for them.

<Callout title="Mantle feature: Billing models">

Pay attention to how customers already expect to pay: monthly subscription, usage-based pricing, seats, credits, one-time purchase, or paid pilot. You do not need final pricing yet, but you do need to know what feels normal in the category.

</Callout>

## Use pricing as context, not strategy

Do not blindly copy your competitors' pricing.

This happens a lot in app ecosystems, especially Shopify apps. A founder looks at three competitors, picks a similar price, and assumes that must be the market.

But you do not know if that pricing is working for them. You do not know if customers think it is too expensive, too cheap, confusing, or only worth it because of a feature you are not planning to build.

Use competitor pricing as context, not strategy.

The goal is to understand what customers value enough to pay for, what they expect to pay, and where those options still fall short.

## Spot the gap

A useful gap is specific.

Not:

> The market needs something simpler.

Better:

> Existing tools are built for larger teams, but solo Shopify app founders need a lightweight way to understand trial conversion and churn without setting up a full analytics stack.

And "simpler" can mean a lot of things. Sometimes people will happily pay more for a tool that is easier to use, better designed, less clunky, or more focused on their actual workflow.

When you're looking at competitors, remember: just because they solve the problem one way does not mean you need to solve it the same way.

You do not know if their workflow is the best workflow. You do not know if their features are the right features. You do not know if users are happy with the way the problem is being solved.

That's why copying competitors keeps you behind. You get the surface-level app without the customer insight, data, or context that shaped it.

Look for the segment everyone else is ignoring, the workflow existing tools make too hard, the complaint that never gets fixed, the customer who would pay more for a better experience, or the workaround that is annoying but still deeply used.

## Find your angle

After you look at competitors, alternatives, reviews, pricing, and workarounds, you're looking for the reason your app could be useful in a way the current options are not.

Not:

> We're cheaper.

Not:

> We use AI.

Not:

> We're easier to use.

Unless you can explain easier for who, doing what, and why the current options make it harder than it needs to be.

A stronger angle sounds more specific:

> Existing project management tools help teams organize work, but freelance web and UX designers still struggle to track client deliverables across multiple Framer projects. This app can win by being the lightweight deliverable tracker built specifically for independent designers.

The point is to name what the market is missing and who you can serve better than the general tools.

You're not writing final positioning yet. You're getting specific about where your app could fit.

## Action item

Open your working chat and talk through these questions. Voice mode is easiest, but typing works too.

If you don't know the answer, say **"I don't know yet."** Don't force an answer just to fill the page. The gaps are useful because they tell you what to research, validate, or ask real customers next.

1. What products, apps, or solutions would someone immediately compare this against?
2. What are people using today instead of a dedicated app?
3. Could someone solve this with ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, a spreadsheet, or a messy internal workflow?
4. What complaints keep showing up in reviews, Reddit threads, communities, forums, TikTok, YouTube comments, or app store reviews?
5. What are customers already paying for?
6. What pricing expectations or support expectations are showing up?
7. Where does the current market feel frustrating, underserved, expensive, too complex, too shallow, too clunky, or too generic?
8. What angle might exist for your app?
