<Callout title="Goal">Identify the most realistic path to your first 10–50 users before you shape the app too tightly.</Callout>

This is the early distribution check: how might people actually find this?

<Callout title="Note">

We'll do a full growth manual later, so we're not building a complete go-to-market strategy here. For now, we're just looking for a believable path to your first users.

</Callout>

Distribution matters now because it can shape the app. A marketplace app, founder-led product, direct outreach product, and social-first consumer app may all need different packaging, onboarding, proof, and first-value moments.

You don't need the whole plan yet. You just need to know whether there's a realistic way to reach the first 10–50 people.

## Start with how people discover tools

Think about where your customer already goes when they need a solution.

| Customer | Where they might look first |
| --- | --- |
| Shopify merchants | Shopify App Store, Shopify Community, app reviews, partner recommendations |
| Framer designers | Framer plugins, templates, Framer Community, X, YouTube tutorials |
| Notion users | Notion templates, creator marketplaces, YouTube, Reddit, newsletters |
| Chrome users | Chrome Web Store, Google search, Reddit, product demos |
| WordPress users | WordPress plugin directory, Google search, YouTube, agency recommendations |
| Consumer app users | TikTok, Instagram, app stores, creators, paid ads, friends |

Ecosystem products usually have a built-in discovery path, which is useful because the customer already knows where to look. But built-in discovery does not mean easy discovery. There are more apps, templates, tools, and AI products launching every day, so you still need a clear customer, clear problem, strong proof, and a reason someone should choose you.

## Add a research pass

This is a good place to use deep research.

Ask your AI tool to look for where your target customer discovers tools, asks for recommendations, complains about current solutions, and talks about the problem.

You can also search manually in places like Reddit, YouTube comments, app store reviews, TikTok, LinkedIn, Slack groups, Discords, Facebook groups, and niche communities.

You're looking for patterns like:

- where people ask "what tool should I use for this?"
- where people complain about the current options
- who they trust for recommendations
- what language they use to describe the problem
- whether they already search for this kind of solution

## Compare possible channels

Some channels are easier to start today. Others are powerful, but take more effort, trust, or time.

| Channel | Best for | Can you start before product? | Effort |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Founder-led marketing | Building authority and trust around the problem | Yes | Low to medium |
| Communities | Learning from and helping your target users | Yes | Low to medium |
| Direct outreach | Getting early conversations and feedback | Yes | Medium |
| SEO / AEO content | Capturing people already searching for answers | Yes | Medium |
| Marketplace discovery | Ecosystem apps, plugins, templates, and extensions | Partly | Medium |
| Lead magnet | Testing interest and collecting early demand | Yes | Low to medium |
| Social content | Consumer apps, creator-led products, visual products, or trendy tools | Yes | Medium |
| Partnerships, affiliates, agencies, or creators | Borrowing trust from people who already reach your audience | Usually later | High |
| Paid ads | Testing specific offers or scaling what already works | Usually later | Medium to high |

You do not need to do all of these. Please do not do all of these.

You're looking for the channel that best matches your customer, app, and stage.

## Think about product-led, sales-led, or founder-led

You don't need to decide your entire business model right now, but it helps to think about how someone would realistically try and buy this.

A **product-led** motion means users can find the app, understand it, try it, and get value without talking to you first. This works best when the app is easy to explain, easy to start, and the value shows up quickly.

A **sales-led** motion means the app probably needs more trust, education, setup, customization, approval, or a higher price point before someone buys. This is more common when the buyer is different from the end user, the workflow is complex, or the app touches important business operations.

A lot of early apps are actually **founder-led** before they become either one. You might do manual outreach, onboarding calls, demos, support, or concierge setup just to learn what people need and get the first users in.

That's normal. Even if you eventually want a self-serve app, founder-led work might be the fastest way to learn.

<Callout title="Mantle feature: Early CRM">

Founder-led does not mean untracked. Even if your first 10 users come from DMs, Reddit, LinkedIn, or warm intros, capture who they are, what they care about, where they came from, and what they asked for. That becomes your early CRM, lifecycle, and product signal.

</Callout>

## Start before the app exists

Some distribution work does not require a finished app.

You can start building trust before the app exists by answering questions in communities, posting about the problem, sharing research, creating a checklist or template, collecting waitlist signups, publishing SEO/AEO content, reaching out to people with the pain, or documenting what you're learning publicly.

Founder-led marketing is especially underrated here. You can become known for understanding the problem before you have the finished solution.

For example, I answer questions publicly on Reddit as myself because I genuinely like helping people. Most people don't do that. It stands out because it feels human, and it has actually led to consulting inquiries for my service business.

## Let the channel shape the app

Distribution is not separate from the app.

The way people find your app should match how they want to try, trust, and use it. If people find you in the Shopify App Store, your listing, screenshots, reviews, pricing, onboarding, and setup flow matter a lot. If people find you through Reddit or founder-led content, trust and helpfulness matter more than polish. If people find you through TikTok, creators, or social content, the app needs to be easy to explain, show, and try quickly.

The channel should not fully dictate the app, but it should influence how you package it, explain it, and get people to the first useful moment.

## Know what the first 10–50 users might look like

Your first users probably won't come from a magical launch moment. They'll usually come from manual, slightly unscalable work.

That might look like:

- 20 thoughtful DMs to people with the pain
- 5 customer interviews from a niche community
- 10 waitlist signups from a lead magnet
- 3 beta users from Reddit or LinkedIn
- 1 agency partner who introduces you to clients
- 5 merchants from Shopify Community
- 10 people who reply to your founder-led posts

This is not the forever strategy. It's the first motion. The goal is to understand whether you can reach the right people at all.

## Action item

Open your working chat and talk through these questions. Voice mode is easiest, but typing works too.

If you don't know the answer, say **"I don't know yet."** The gaps are useful because they tell you what to research next.

1. Where does your target customer already go to find tools, templates, advice, or solutions?
2. Is there an ecosystem or marketplace where they would naturally search first?
3. Where do they spend time when they are not actively shopping for tools?
4. Are there communities, Reddit threads, Slack groups, Facebook groups, newsletters, creators, agencies, or partners that already reach them?
5. Could you start building trust before the app exists?
6. What could you post, share, answer, or create this week?
7. Is this more likely to start product-led, sales-led, founder-led, or a mix?
8. What would your first 10–50 users realistically come from?
9. Which channel feels most realistic to test first?
