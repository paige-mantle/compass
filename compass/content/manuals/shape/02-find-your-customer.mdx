<Callout title="Goal">Choose the first customer group to understand, validate with, and build for.</Callout>

Narrowing down who your app could serve is non-negotiable if you want to build something people actually buy.

You do not need to know every customer this app could eventually serve. You need to know who to start with.

"Small businesses." "Busy professionals." "Developers." These sound specific until you actually try to build or market something for them. They are categories, not customers.

You can't write good landing page copy for a category. You can't target a category. And you probably won't build something a category feels deeply understood by.

One of my biggest pieces of advice when figuring out your first customer is to niche down. Make it more specific than feels comfortable.

The broader your audience, the harder it is to sell your app. Your messaging gets broader. Your pricing gets more vague. Your marketing gets more expensive because you're trying to reach too many different people with too many different needs.

Niching down makes validation easier because you know exactly who to talk to. It makes messaging easier because you can use their language. And it makes it easier to get someone to pay because the app feels like it was built for their actual situation, not a vague audience segment.

The founders who get traction fastest usually start smaller: one specific type of person, one specific painful problem, then expand from there.

## Define the likely customer

In your working chat, list all the types of people, teams, or potential end users that might have the problem you defined in 1.1.

Name anyone who comes to mind. The obvious groups, the adjacent groups, and the weirdly specific groups. You're not choosing yet. You're making the options visible.

For example, "freelancers" is a place to start, but it is not specific enough to build for.

A more useful version might be:

> Freelance web and UX designers in North America who ship client sites in Framer and manage more than four active client projects at a time.

That is specific enough that you can start to picture their tools, workflow, context, and where the pain shows up.

When you're narrowing the customer, look at things like role, business type, company size, location, age range if it matters, tools they already use, workflow maturity, budget, and how often they feel the pain.

| Broad group | More useful customer description |
| --- | --- |
| Freelancers | Freelance web and UX designers in North America who ship client sites in Framer and manage 4+ active client projects |
| Agencies | Small Shopify agencies that manage client reporting manually across spreadsheets, Slack, and recurring client calls |
| Founders | Solo SaaS founders who handle onboarding, support, and billing questions themselves |

The useful version is specific enough that you could find a real person who matches it.

## The end user isn't always the buyer

The person who feels the pain is not always the person who pays.

Sometimes the end user and the buyer are the same person. A solo founder or consumer buys the tool, uses it every day, and pays for it. Great. Clean.

Sometimes they're different, especially in B2B.

- A manager researches and buys the tool → the operations team lives in it
- A parent pays for the app → the child uses it
- A Shopify store owner approves the subscription → the junior marketer uses the app every week

This matters because buyers and users care about different things. The buyer cares about budget, risk, trust, and business value. The end user cares about whether the app makes their actual day less annoying.

If there are multiple people you need to attract, convince, or support, map them out now. It will change your positioning, messaging, pricing, onboarding, and probably the app itself.

<Callout title="Mantle feature: Customer records">

If the buyer and user are different, track that early. It affects onboarding, pricing, billing, support, lifecycle emails, and the way you measure whether an account is healthy.

</Callout>

<Callout title="Note">

If the end user signs up for the free plan and loves it, but their manager doesn't see the value, you have adoption without revenue.

If the buyer likes the value, but the user hates the workflow or UX, you have churn waiting politely in the corner.

</Callout>

## Segment by situation

A customer gets more useful when you describe their situation, not just their title.

"Freelance designer" is okay. "Freelance web and UX designer in North America who builds client sites in Framer and manages 4+ active projects at a time" is much better.

The situation tells you why the pain matters now. It also helps you avoid building for someone who technically has the problem but does not feel it often enough to care.

This is where tools, platforms, and ecosystems start to matter too. A Framer designer, a Webflow designer, a Squarespace designer, and a Wix designer might all have a similar problem, but they are not necessarily the same customer. They use different tools, hang out in different places, expect different workflows, and may have very different willingness to pay.

We'll go deeper on platform and ecosystem choices in **1.5 Shape the product**, but for now, use them to sharpen the customer.

| Customer | Situation | Pain | Current workflow | Urgency |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Freelance Framer designer | Managing 4+ active client projects in North America | Loses track of deliverables and revision requests | Framer, Notion, Slack, email, memory | High when deadlines overlap |
| Freelance Webflow designer | Managing multiple client sites with ongoing support work | Struggles to track requests after launch | Webflow, email, Trello, Google Docs | Medium to high |
| Small Shopify agency | Managing recurring client reporting manually | Spends too much time pulling updates together | Shopify, spreadsheets, Slack, client calls | High near reporting deadlines |

You are not trying to make every segment look equally good. You are trying to see which one has the clearest pain, the clearest workflow, and the clearest path to reach them.

## Find the highest-pain group

Now choose the group with the strongest signal.

Look for three things: they feel the pain often, they have a reason to pay, and you can actually reach them.

Pain matters because polite interest does not build a business. Willingness to pay matters because "I would totally use this" is not the same as "here is my card." Reachability matters because a perfect customer you cannot find is not a starting market.

This is especially useful if the same app idea could serve multiple ecosystems or customer types. The same tool might technically work for Framer designers, Webflow designers, Squarespace designers, and Wix designers, but that does not mean they are equally good starting points.

Compare them. Who has the strongest pain? Who already spends money on tools? Who is easiest to find? Who is already complaining about the problem in places like Reddit, communities, reviews, or forums?

We'll go deeper on market research in **1.3 Map the market**, but for now, you're looking for the group that gives you the best shot at an early win.

Pick your first useful customer. Not your forever customer.

<RealityCheck>

If you are building for everyone, you are probably building for no one yet.

</RealityCheck>

## Check buying power

A painful problem still needs a believable path to payment.

When you start talking to potential end users, ask who controls the budget, who researches tools, and who actually approves the purchase.

In B2B, these may all be different people. The person feeling the pain might be the end user. The person comparing options might be the internal champion or evaluator. The person approving the spend might be the buyer.

This matters even more in app ecosystems. People building ecommerce stores on Shopify and people building ecommerce stores on Wix may technically need similar apps. There are app developers who build the same core functionality for both ecosystems, but the buyers are not the same.

A Shopify merchant is often more advanced, more revenue-focused, and more used to paying for apps that improve conversion, operations, or retention. A Wix user may be earlier-stage, more price-sensitive, and more likely to expect a lower-cost tool.

Same app idea. Different customer. Different willingness to pay. Different positioning. Different pricing.

This is not final pricing work. That comes later. This is just a gut check so you do not spend weeks building for people who love the idea but have no budget, no authority, and no urgency.

If the buyer is unclear, write that down now. It is much easier to fix before you build than after your waitlist is full of people who cannot pay.

Add a side-by-side screenshot comparison here: one Shopify merchant or app founder talking about an app being "cheap" at a higher price point, beside a Wix or Framer user complaining about pricing at a much lower price point. Same broad category, totally different customer expectations.

</Callout>

## Find where they hang out

Your first customer group should be findable.

That might mean LinkedIn search, niche Slack groups, Reddit threads, Shopify Community posts, app marketplace reviews, Discord groups, Facebook groups, newsletters, podcasts, or small events.

A 200-person Slack group full of the exact customer is more useful than a 50,000-person generic entrepreneur community.

Find the specific room, not the biggest one.

| Customer group | Where they might hang out |
| --- | --- |
| Freelance Framer designers | Framer Community, design Twitter/X, Reddit, portfolio communities |
| Shopify merchants | Shopify Community, r/shopify, app reviews, ecommerce Facebook groups |
| Wix site owners | Wix Community, Facebook groups, small business forums |
| Small Shopify agencies | Partner communities, LinkedIn, agency Slack groups, Shopify meetups |

## Choose your first customer group

Now take your notes and organize them into one clear starting customer group.

You do not need to write a perfect positioning statement yet. You just need enough clarity to know who you are going to validate with first.

Ask your AI tool to organize your notes into the customer group, their situation, the pain, what they do today, who the end user is, who the buyer is if different, where you can find them, and why this group is a strong starting point.

The goal is to move from "this could be for a lot of people" to "this is the first group I'm going to understand deeply."

Example:

> The first customer group is freelance web and UX designers in North America who build client sites in Framer and manage more than four active client projects. They struggle to track deliverables, revision requests, and deadlines across Framer, Notion, Slack, and email. They are also the end user and buyer, which makes early validation simpler. They are findable in Framer communities, design Twitter/X, LinkedIn, and freelance design groups.

That is specific enough to build for. Specific enough to write for. And specific enough to find.

## Action item

Open your working chat and talk through these questions. Voice mode is easiest, but typing works too.

If you don't know the answer, say **"I don't know yet."** Don't force an answer just to fill the page. The gaps are useful because they tell you what to research, validate, or ask real customers next.

1. Who might have the problem you defined in 1.1?
2. Which groups are too broad?
3. Which groups can you describe by role, situation, tools, location, company size, budget, or workflow?
4. Who is the end user?
5. Who is the buyer, if different?
6. Which group feels the pain most clearly?
7. Which group has a believable path to payment?
8. Where can you find real people who match?
9. Which group should you validate with first?
