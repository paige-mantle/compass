**Goal:** Prove the pain is real.

Every useful app starts with a real problem people are already dealing with. Not a feature idea or a cool use of AI. This comes first because you can't validate demand for an app nobody knows exists yet. You can only validate demand for the problem.

If you can't clearly explain that problem from the perspective of your ideal customer, everything downstream will get really hard: positioning, onboarding, marketing, and eventually the product itself.

## Identify the pain

First up, let's identify the pain your potential user is struggling with.

Brain dump every pain point you think your app could solve. Don't hold back.

You don't need to worry about features yet or how the app works. Just focus on the actual frustration people are dealing with, because AI might come up with a better product solution than the one you had in mind anyway.

Unless you're solving your own problem (*highly recommend this*), these are still assumptions until you talk to real people. Best guess for now. We'll validate later.

| Type | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Too vague | I want to build a project management tool for freelancers. |
| Useful | Freelancers lose track of client deliverables when juggling more than three projects and miss deadlines. |

## Spot when it happens

For each pain point, think about when it shows up.

The timing matters because it gives you the start of the workflow. It tells you what triggers the friction and where your app might eventually fit.

This part is harder to guess, especially if you're building a B2B app. Talk to end users and have them walk you through what they're doing in a real day. Workflows are getting less standardized now that more people use AI in their day-to-day work. One customer might rely on MCP integrations and automations, while another is still copy-pasting between five tabs. The workflow is usually where the actual pain shows up.

## Look at what they do today

Now look at what people are already using to solve the problem.

Common workarounds include spreadsheets, Zapier flows, manual processes, copying and pasting between tools, or duct-taping disconnected apps together because there's no direct integration.

Sometimes you're not creating a brand-new behavior. You're improving something people already do. In that case, the useful question is: how are they doing it now, and how would it change with your app?

<FieldNote title="IRL example: tiny tool, huge relief">

I've been loving a design tool called Paper. They recently launched Snapshot, a Chrome extension that lets you capture elements from a landing page, then paste them into Paper as editable frames.

On paper, pun intended, it sounds almost too easy: capture something, paste it, edit the prototype. But it has saved me hours and hours of time. If I had to go without it now, my workflow would genuinely get worse.

</FieldNote>

That's the kind of signal you're looking for. The app does not have to sound complicated to be valuable. It just has to remove real friction from something people are already trying to do.

## Name the cost

A problem gets more useful when you can name what it costs the customer.

And no, we're not just talking about the price tag. Cost can mean time, missed revenue, mistakes, stress, customer trust, team confusion, risk, or the mental tax of doing the same annoying thing over and over.

This is the difference between "this is annoying" and "this is annoying enough to fix." In the Paper example above, the cost is time. And time is money, especially when the old workflow eats hours you could have spent doing higher-value work.

## Write the problem statement

At this point, you don't need to force a perfect sentence by yourself. That's what the AI pass is for.

Capture the raw pieces first: who feels the pain, when it happens, what they do today, what it costs them, and what they actually want instead. Then you can ask AI to turn that into a cleaner problem statement.

Use this format:

> [Customer] struggles with [problem] when [situation], which causes [cost / friction / missed opportunity].

If you can't get the problem into one sentence after organizing your notes, you're probably not ready to build yet.

## Define the outcome

A good problem statement explains what's broken. A good outcome explains what the customer wants instead.

This is where the product marketing work starts to get useful. A simple feature-benefit matrix can help you connect the pain to the outcome, and later to the features you may actually build. April Dunford uses this kind of feature/value/benefit thinking in positioning work, and it's useful here because it keeps you from jumping straight from "pain" to "random feature idea."

For now, keep it simple:

| Pain point | Job to be done | Desired outcome | Possible feature |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Freelancers lose track of client deliverables when juggling more than three projects. | Stay on top of what is due, late, and at risk across active clients. | Freelancers know exactly what needs attention without checking five different places. | Leave this blank for now, or add ideas later. |

Don't overcomplicate this. Just ask:

- What are they trying to get done?
- What do they want to avoid?
- What would "fixed" look like?

This helps you avoid building random features and focus on the result your app actually needs to create.

## Action item

Open your working chat and talk through these questions. Voice mode is easiest, but typing works too.

If you don't know the answer, say **"I don't know yet."** Don't force an answer just to fill the page. The gaps are useful because they tell you what to research, validate, or ask real customers next.

1. What pain points do you think your app could solve?
2. When does each pain point show up?
3. What are people doing today to solve or avoid the problem?
4. What does the problem cost them?
5. What would the customer actually want instead?
6. What possible features came to mind, even if you're not ready to build them yet?
