**Goal:** Define the basic brand pieces that will guide your first build, landing page, and app experience.

Let's come up with some brand basics. Not a full design system yet. We'll go deeper on branding later.

For now, we're making the early decisions that help your app feel intentional: name, personality, voice, visual direction, typography, logo, and app icon.

Some things are easy to change later. A headline can change. A section can change. A button label can change.

Other things are harder to reverse, especially your product name, domain, app icon, and the general direction people start associating with your app.

## Name the product

Your name is not your whole brand, but it is one of the decisions that can be harder to change later.

It's how people find it, remember it, recommend it, and say it out loud without feeling weird.

I'm personally a naming nerd. I love naming products. But the perfect product name does not exist, and you should not spend three weeks in a naming spiral.

### What makes a good product name

A good name is easy to say, easy to spell, available enough to use, not too limiting if the product expands later, and short enough to work in app stores, marketplaces, and search results.

Two-word names are fine, but if you can make it one clear, memorable word, that's usually easier to say, search, and remember.

### Naming mistakes to avoid

| Naming mistake | Why it causes problems | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Too specific | It can limit the product if it expands later | Athletic Greens becoming AG1 gives the brand more room than "greens" |
| Hard to spell | People hear it, then guess wrong when they search | Klaviyo is memorable, but the spelling is not obvious the first time you hear it |
| Hard to pronounce | People hesitate to say it out loud or recommend it | Any name where people ask, "Wait, how do you say that?" |
| Too long | It takes up space in app stores, listings, and search results | A long Shopify app name leaves less room for useful keywords |
| Weird spelling | You end up constantly correcting people | Dropped vowels, forced misspellings, or names that look like typos |
| AI-generated generic name | It sounds like every other weekend project | "TaskPilot," "Flowly," "NexusAI," etc. |
| Trend-chasing | It can date the product quickly | Naming everything after household objects, minerals, animals, or random nouns because it's trendy |

<RealityCheck>

Do not use the random name your AI builder gives you. It is usually generic, forgettable, and already sounds like 40 other apps.

</RealityCheck>

### Check availability and associations

Do a basic name search before you get attached.

Search the name in Google, app stores, social platforms, and the category you're building in. Look for similar products, bad associations, confusing meanings, or anything that could make the name harder to own.

This is not legal advice, but check the naming and trademark rules in your country. In general, you do not want a product name that is already being used by another company in the same or closely related category.

If you're building in an ecosystem, check the platform rules too. Shopify, Apple, Google, Chrome, WordPress, and other platforms may have character limits, trademark rules, or restrictions around what you can include in your app name.

### Check the domain and handles

Try to get the `.com` if you can. If you can't, `.app`, `.dev`, and `.io` are common options for software products.

You can also add a short word before or after the name:

- `hey[name].com`
- `try[name].com`
- `get[name].com`
- `[name]app.com`

Check social handles too, especially if founder-led marketing, social content, or community distribution is part of your plan.

### Use it in a sentence

One of my favourite naming tests: use the name like a real person would.

Try saying:

> "I'm using [name] to track client deliverables."

> "Can you send that to me in [name]?"

> "[Name] helps freelance designers stay on top of client work."

If it feels awkward, hard to say, or like you have to explain it every time, it's probably not the one.

## Define the brand personality

Brand personality matters early because it affects who you attract.

Before someone reads your full landing page or tries your product, they're getting signals from the name, colors, logo, typography, screenshots, and copy. Those signals tell them whether this feels like it was made for them.

A sharp developer tool can be playful and colorful if you want it to be, but that choice should be intentional. If your target user expects precision, speed, and control, a brand that feels like a cheerful consumer app might create the wrong expectation.

Ask:

- Who is going to see this first?
- What do they already trust?
- What would excite them?
- What would make them think, "this is for me"?
- How do you want them to feel when they come across the product?

Pick three to five words that describe the personality.

| Product direction | Personality words |
| --- | --- |
| Calm operations tool | Clear, steady, practical, trustworthy |
| Playful consumer app | Bright, social, optimistic, casual |
| Developer tool | Sharp, technical, precise, fast |
| Founder analytics tool | Direct, confident, simple, useful |
| Design workflow tool | Polished, visual, smart, lightweight |

Your product name can influence this too. A playful name might give you room for puns, personality, or a memorable color direction. A more serious name might push the brand toward trust, precision, and restraint.

## Set the brand voice

Brand voice is how the product sounds.

It matters in content marketing, but it also matters inside the product. Onboarding, empty states, errors, upgrade prompts, tooltips, and buttons all shape how the product feels.

The voice should make sense for your end user. A consumer app for Gen Z can sound completely different from a B2B finance tool for enterprise CFOs.

Define how formal or casual it should sound, whether it uses humor, what words it should avoid, and how it should explain errors, empty states, onboarding steps, and upgrade prompts.

A quick version:

> The voice should be clear, helpful, and direct. It can have a little personality, but it should not sound cute, corporate, or like a motivational LinkedIn post.

## Choose the basic visual direction

Now choose enough visual direction for the first version.

Not the full design system. Just the basics: primary color, secondary or accent color, background direction, typography, logo or wordmark direction, app icon direction, border radius, imagery style, and rules for gradients, shadows, and motion.

<Callout title="Prompt idea">

Based on my brand personality, brand voice, target customer, product category, and estimated user demographics, what visual direction would make sense? Suggest color palettes, typography direction, logo/icon direction, and visual styles to avoid.

</Callout>

A simple starter direction might be:

> Use a warm off-white background, one confident accent color, clean sans-serif type, a simple mono label style, soft borders, minimal shadows, and no heavy gradients.

That is enough to guide a first build.

## Pick typography

Fonts do a lot of brand work, but you do not need final type choices yet.

| Type role | Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Heading font | Clean sans-serif, slightly editorial, or more distinctive |
| Body font | Highly readable, neutral, easy to scan |
| Mono font | Optional, useful for labels, metrics, code, or system details |
| UI font | Clear, compact, works well in buttons, nav, and forms |

Google Fonts are totally fine at this stage. You do not need to buy a custom type system before you know if the product has legs.

Do not pick a beautiful font that makes the product harder to use. Pretty is nice. Readable is non-negotiable.

## Create logo and app icon basics

Your logo matters, but your app icon might matter even more, especially if you're building inside an app store, extension marketplace, browser, dashboard, or mobile environment.

It shows up in app stores, browser tabs, sidebars, dashboards, mobile screens, integrations, Slack previews, emails, and all the tiny places where your full brand does not fit.

<RealityCheck>

Do not use the default app icon from your builder. Lovable, Base44, and other AI builders often generate generic gradient icons that look like every other weekend project. It's a tiny trust leak before someone even clicks.

</RealityCheck>

You do not need a final logo yet, but you should define the direction: wordmark only, symbol only, symbol + wordmark, simple app icon, mascot or character, initials, abstract mark, or platform-friendly icon style.

<Callout title="App icon workflow">

Use Gemini or ChatGPT to generate a simple flat app icon concept. Keep it minimal, legible, and not too detailed. Then bring it into Figma and turn it into a cleaner vector using a vectorize plugin or manual cleanup.

</Callout>

The first version can be simple. It just needs to be recognizable, legible at small sizes, and not identical to everything else in the category.

## Write the starter one-liner

You do not need final positioning yet, but you do need one plain sentence that explains what this is.

Use the work you've already done:

> [Product] helps [specific customer] [do the job] so they can [get the outcome].

Example:

> [Product] helps freelance Framer designers track client deliverables so they can stay ahead of deadlines without checking five different places.

This is not forever copy. It is a working sentence you can use in your landing page, product brief, and builder prompt.
