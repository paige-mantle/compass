<Callout title="Goal">Find out whether the right people care enough to take action.</Callout>

You don't need a prototype or finished app to validate demand. In fact, this should happen upfront, before you spend too much time, energy, and credits building the wrong thing.

With AI research, deep research, landing pages, lightweight prototypes, and direct outreach, it's pretty fast to run a quick validation test. You're not trying to prove the whole business yet. You're trying to find out whether real people care enough about the problem to do something.

That action might be a reply, a call, an email, a waitlist signup, a manual sale, a pre-order, or a real payment. The stronger the action, the stronger the signal.

## Choose the biggest guess

Before you test demand, figure out the biggest thing you're still guessing about.

That might be:

- whether the customer actually has the problem
- whether the problem is painful enough to act on
- whether they would pay for a better solution
- whether the current workaround is already good enough
- whether the platform or ecosystem will let the app work the way you need
- whether they trust you enough to try it

This matters because you don't want to test everything at once. You want to test the assumption that could break the idea if it turns out to be wrong.

For example:

> The biggest guess is that freelance web and UX designers care enough about managing Framer project deliverables to join a waitlist or book a quick call.

Or:

> The biggest guess is that Shopify merchants are frustrated enough with their current reporting workflow to try a new app.

Compliments are nice, but behavior is better: replies, signups, booked calls, payments, or someone taking the next step without being dragged there.

## Ways to test demand quickly

There are a few ways to test demand quickly. Some are fast and lightweight. Others take more effort, but give you stronger signal.

The goal is to pick the smallest test that can answer your biggest guess.

| Test | Best for | Strong signal looks like | Effort |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Deep research | Finding complaints, patterns, language, and proof | The same pain shows up across reviews, Reddit, forums, app stores, TikTok, YouTube comments, or communities | Low |
| Direct outreach | Seeing if real people will reply or agree to talk | People respond, share details, or agree to a call without a huge pitch | Medium |
| Customer interviews | Understanding the pain, workflow, and current workaround | People describe the problem before you over-explain it | Medium |
| Community or social post | Testing whether the pain resonates publicly | Strangers reply with "yes, this happens to me" and share their own examples | Low to medium |
| Lead magnet | Testing whether people want help solving the problem | People comment, DM, or give an email to get the checklist, template, teardown, calculator, or guide | Low to medium |
| Landing page or waitlist | Testing whether the message is clear enough for action | Relevant people join the waitlist, click the CTA, or ask when it's available | Medium |
| Prototype walkthrough | Testing whether the workflow makes sense | People understand the flow, ask smart questions, or want to try it with their own data | Medium to high |
| Concierge/manual version | Testing the outcome before you automate it | Someone lets you do the work manually or asks for the result again | High |
| Pre-sell or paid pilot | Testing willingness to pay | Someone pays, asks for an invoice, or agrees to a paid pilot | Highest |

Research is useful, but don't stop there. The stronger signal is when someone replies, signs up, books a call, shares the post, asks for the resource, or pays.

## Pick one test

Choose one test that answers your biggest guess.

If you need to understand the pain, start with direct outreach or customer interviews. If you need to test whether the message lands, use a landing page, waitlist, community post, or lead magnet. If you need to test whether the workflow creates value, use a prototype walkthrough or concierge version. If you need to test willingness to pay, try a pre-sell or paid pilot.

Don't run six soft tests and call the confusion "mixed signal." Pick one clear test, decide what strong signal looks like, and run it properly.

## Create the smallest proof asset

A proof asset is the thing you use to test demand.

It might be a landing page, waitlist form, short Loom, clickable prototype, community post, one-page offer, manual service version, lead magnet, or Stripe payment link.

<Callout title="Mantle feature: Customer segmentation">

A waitlist is not just an email list. It is your first customer system. Tag where people came from, what pain they mentioned, what they clicked, and whether they are a likely buyer, user, partner, or early beta customer.

</Callout>

The asset should make the promise clear enough for someone to react. It does not need to be pretty. It needs to be specific.

## Talk to real people

The highest-signal work is usually a real conversation where you ask about past behavior, not hypothetical "would you use this?" future behavior.

Good questions sound like:

- When was the last time this happened?
- What did you do when it happened?
- What tools or workarounds did you use?
- What did it cost you?
- Have you paid to solve this before?

People are generous with hypothetical yeses. They are more honest about what they already did.

## Track real signals

Not all validation is equal.

Someone paying is stronger than someone booking a call. Someone booking a call is stronger than someone joining a waitlist. Someone joining a waitlist is stronger than someone saying "interesting."

"Interesting" is not validation. It is politeness wearing a blazer.

## Decide what the signal means

Before you run the test, decide what strong signal looks like. Otherwise, it's too easy to call anything "promising" because you want to keep building.

| Test | Example goal |
| --- | --- |
| Direct outreach | 5 replies or 3 calls from 20 targeted messages |
| Community or social post | Strangers reply with their own version of the problem |
| Lead magnet | People comment, DM, or give their email to get it |
| Landing page or waitlist | Relevant visitors join the waitlist or click the CTA |
| Prototype walkthrough | People understand the flow and want to try it with their own data |
| Pre-sell or paid pilot | Someone pays, asks for an invoice, or agrees to a paid pilot |

Weak signal does not always mean the idea is dead. It might mean the customer is too broad, the pain is not urgent, the message is unclear, or the test reached the wrong people.

But do not ignore weak signal just because you want to keep building.

## Action item

Open your working chat and talk through these questions. Voice mode is easiest, but typing works too.

If you don't know the answer, say **"I don't know yet."** Don't force an answer just to fill the page. The gaps are useful because they tell you what to research, validate, or ask real customers next.

1. What is the biggest thing you're still guessing about?
2. What would need to be true for this app idea to work?
3. What is the smallest test you could run this week?
4. What proof asset do you need to create?
5. Who will you test it with?
6. What would strong signal look like?
7. What would weak signal tell you?
8. What will you do next based on the result?
